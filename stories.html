Stories & personal lens

Why this fight is personal

Before I saw betting apps on every screen, I watched a relative fall into debt chasing “one big win.” That experience shapes how I hear casual campus jokes about parlays and “free money.”

How the harm starts What we can change

Takeaway

Gambling rarely feels dangerous until the losses arrive. We have to tell people the risks sooner.
Family story

From small bets to stolen credit cards

A relative of mine started with five-dollar wagers after work. A few lucky wins convinced him he was “good” at betting. Then those small bets turned to large ones, and his “winnings” turned to losses. Desperate to recover, he opened credit cards that weren’t his. My family intervened before he lost everything, but the experience made something clear: gambling feels safe right up until it doesn’t.

On-campus snapshots

What I see among peers

“It’s not gambling if you know sports.”

Friends insist they have an “edge” because they watch every game. Ads reinforce that overconfidence by calling bets “investments.”

Peer pressure in group chats

Odds and parlays circulate like memes. Saying “I’m out” can feel awkward, so people download apps just to stay in the conversation.

Fantasy as a gateway

Daily fantasy contests blur into betting. Because fantasy is framed as strategy, students assume the same skills will beat house odds.

What I wish people heard earlier

Take these with you

Winning early isn’t a sign of skill

A few hits are often luck. Overconfidence makes people raise stakes right before probability catches up.

“Responsible gambling” tools are mostly optics

Limits are easy to bypass. Apps are designed to keep you playing, not to make stopping easy.

Opting out is an option

You don’t owe anyone a parlay. Suggest a watch party, a pickup game, or a non-betting fantasy league instead.
